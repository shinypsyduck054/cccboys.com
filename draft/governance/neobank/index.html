WHERE THE·MONEY·LIVES

Per-product accounts. CCC Boys rails. Wage-first ordering, encoded.
Governance › Neobank

THE IDEA IN PLAIN TERMS

Every CCC Boys product, every app, every depot, every service line, has its own dedicated account. Money that comes in for that product (Customer unlocks, a Virtual Member's share for that month, a sale of recycled shingles, a hurricane prep contract) lands in that product's account.

From there, the money flows out in a fixed order. Wages first. Operating costs second. Reserves third. Cross-subsidy to the corps general fund fourth. Surplus to the people who built it last. The order is not negotiable in the moment. It is written into the rails.

This is not exotic. Cooperatives have done this with paper ledgers for a century. The difference is that ours runs on a programmable rail (the Dakota neobank), so the ordering executes itself and the ledger is auditable in real time by any member.

WHAT THIS MEANS FOR YOU

If you are a Customer
You pay $1 once to unlock a one-shot app, or $1 a month for a living service. Here is where a one-shot dollar goes:
  1. Lands in that app's product account
  2. Joins the wage pool for the Corps Members who built that app
  3. Helps cover the app's small operating cost
  4. A small share goes to the general fund (Apprentice wages, runway, brand)
  5. Anything left becomes Corps Member surplus for that app
Your dollar does five jobs. None of them go to Apple, Google, or a venture investor.
If you are a Virtual Member
You pay $100 a year and get the whole cheap-tier library unlocked, plus a card and a member number. The corps spreads that $100 across the catalog. The math:
  1. Catalog has N cheap-tier products live this year
  2. Your $100 is split across them, each slice landing in that product's account
  3. Each slice runs through the same waterfall above
  4. Your unlock applies to every $1 app and service, current and future
The bigger the catalog grows, the smaller your share per product. The total support you give the corps stays $100 a year.
If you are a Corps Member
You work hours on one or more products. Your wage shows up weekly out of those products' accounts. Surplus tracks separately:
  1. The neobank logs your hours per product
  2. Once the product clears reserve, your share of the surplus is allocated proportional to hours
  3. You can draw surplus quarterly or hold it in the pool to compound
  4. Working on a high-margin app pays surplus faster; working on a building-phase product pays wage on the corps general fund until it clears reserve
Wage first means you do not gamble on whether a product hits. Surplus is the upside on top.
If you are an Apprentice
Your wage is guaranteed by the corps general fund, regardless of which product you happen to be training on this month. You do not bear product risk during apprenticeship. Specifically:
  1. You log hours just like a Member
  2. The product account pays what it can; the general fund tops up to the apprentice wage
  3. You build a surplus claim too, but at the apprentice rate, not the Member rate
  4. When you graduate, the surplus claim converts to Member-rate going forward

THE RAILS

The neobank sits on top of Dakota.xyz, which is the same financial infrastructure Justin and Jabbey worked on together in their prior roles. That gives the corps a fast, cheap, programmable USD rail with stablecoin settlement underneath. No SWIFT delays. No 30% App Store cut. No correspondent bank skim.

On top of the Dakota rail, the corps runs its own bookkeeping layer with three properties that matter:

PER-PRODUCT SUBACCOUNTS
Each product is a separate subaccount of the corps wallet. Incoming Stripe (or direct stablecoin) payments are routed automatically by SKU. Outgoing wage payments are scheduled by hours logged. No human signs off on a routine wage run.
PASSKEY-SIGNED VOTES
Member votes are signed with the same passkey that secures their wage payouts. A vote is verifiable in the ledger. No more meeting-minute discrepancies. No more "I do not remember voting that way." If the ledger has your signature, you voted.
WAGE-FIRST ORDERING ENCODED
The waterfall above is not a policy in a binder. It is a contract in code. The runway reserve cannot be raided. The corps cross-subsidy is automatic. Surplus distribution happens on a fixed schedule. Members can change the rules, but they have to vote to change the code.

WHO BUILDS IT

Jabbey is the Builder who owns the neobank layer. Justin can ship V1 himself as backstop if the timing slips. Either way, the rails ship as part of the corps' Y1 infrastructure so the first $1 Customer unlock can route through them, not through a third-party payment processor with margin attached.
