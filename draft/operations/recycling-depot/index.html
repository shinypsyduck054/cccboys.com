C&D Recycling Depot

Hub-and-spoke construction debris recycling for South Carolina
Research compiled June 29, 2026

The Concept

South Carolina contractors and roofers currently pay to landfill construction and demolition (C&D) debris because no cost-competitive recycling infrastructure exists along the coast. We build that infrastructure as a worker-owned cooperative, pricing below every landfill in the state, and capture a market that has no incumbent.

Why this isn't a bet

Joey's Magnolia Roofing (Member #3) pays to landfill shingles right now. That's confirmed Day 1 feedstock from inside the corps, no sales effort required. No chicken-and-egg problem. Joey's contractor network is the distribution channel. The supply is already there -- it's going to the wrong place.

What We Recycle

Four material streams sorted at the hub:

  • Asphalt shingles -- grind into road base and aggregate filler; secondary market via asphalt plants
  • Metal -- scrap yards, commodity sale; pulled at spokes to reduce haul weight
  • Clean wood -- mulch and biomass buyers
  • Concrete and masonry -- crush into fill aggregate

Hub-and-Spoke Structure

A single main facility handles sorting and processing. Regional spoke sites collect raw material and transfer it to the hub, keeping roofers' haul distances short across the state.

Where to Sort: Hub vs. Spoke

This question is downstream of DHEC permitting because the answer changes the facility classification at each site. Here's the full breakdown:

Option A: Sort Everything at Hub

Pros

  • Spokes need only Transfer Station permits (lighter, faster, cheaper)
  • One DHEC processing relationship to manage
  • Specialized equipment stays at one location
  • Simpler spoke operations -- containers in, containers out

Cons

  • Hub receives mixed loads -- heavier trucks, more fuel cost
  • Hub processing volume is higher -- larger footprint required Day 1
  • Metal revenue flows to hub, not spokes

Option B: Full Sort at Spokes

Pros

  • Cleaner hub loads
  • Metal revenue stays local
  • Reduces hub processing load

Cons

  • Each spoke becomes a processing facility under DHEC -- full Solid Waste Processing Facility permit at every site
  • 5x the permitting burden and cost
  • Inconsistent labor quality across sites for skilled sort work
  • Equipment duplication across all locations

Option C: Hybrid -- Metal Pull at Spokes, Everything Else to Hub

Recommended approach

Spokes pull metal only into a dedicated container (scrap yard picks it up directly). Everything else -- shingles, wood, concrete, mixed -- goes in the hub container. Pulling metal from mixed C&D at a transfer station is separating a clear commodity recyclable, not "processing" in DHEC's framework. Confirm in pre-application conference, but it's a reasonable interpretation. Eliminates 20-30% of hub-bound truckload weight, reduces fuel on every run, captures metal revenue without the permitting burden.

DHEC Permitting

Permitting is the gating dependency before you can lock a site. Everything else -- grants, anchor commitments, site selection -- can run in parallel, but you need to know what DHEC requires before signing a lease.

Hub: Solid Waste Processing Facility Permit

  • Required for any site that mechanically processes C&D material (grinding, sorting, crushing)
  • Process: Pre-application conference with DHEC → formal application → 30-90 day review
  • Application must include facility design, operating plan, stormwater management, financial assurance for closure
  • This is the more complex permit -- start it first

Spokes: Solid Waste Transfer Station Permit

  • Lighter permit -- accept material, hold temporarily, load out to hub
  • Metal commodity separation at the spoke is likely NOT "processing" under DHEC -- confirm in pre-application conference
  • Faster to obtain than a processing permit

SC FastTrack Permitting

SC Commerce runs a state-level FastTrack program requiring participating agencies -- including DHEC -- to complete expedited 30-day reviews instead of standard 90+ day cycles. Qualifications: job creation, capital investment, Tier 3 or 4 county. A recycling facility creating 10+ jobs in a distressed county hits every qualification. FastTrack applies statewide, not county-specific. File via the SC Commerce Business One Stop portal, which lets you file across multiple state agencies simultaneously.

Grant Angle

A worker-owned cooperative bringing recycling jobs to a rural SC county is custom-designed for grant programs. The CCC Boys brand story -- rural SC, 1933 echoes, apprenticeships, trades -- is strong collateral across multiple funders simultaneously.

  • SC rural county incentives: Tier 3/4 job creation programs, property tax abatements, infrastructure support through SC Commerce
  • EPA Solid Waste and Recycling Grants: Direct fit for a C&D diversion facility; EPA has consistently funded infrastructure that diverts construction debris from landfills
  • USDA Rural Development: Cooperative businesses in rural counties are a primary target; can fund building, equipment, and working capital
  • Worker cooperative-specific grants: Multiple national foundations fund cooperative conversions and startups; the CCC Boys structure is a strong match
Timing note

Start grant applications before breaking ground. The DHEC permit application itself is evidence of seriousness -- funders want to see regulatory momentum, not just a pitch. File for grants as soon as you have a site and a pre-application conference date confirmed.

Pricing Strategy

Price below every landfill in the state. Joey's roofing and contractor network handles distribution -- contractors save real money and get a sustainability story they can market to their own clients. The co-op brand turns every dump trip into a positive news beat for the county hosting the facility.

Shingle grinding is the high-margin product. Ground shingles sell into asphalt plants as modified aggregate. The landfill currently captures zero of this value. We capture it and share it with the contractor through a below-market tipping fee.
