A NOTICE FROM SOUTH CAROLINA

SOUTH·CAROLINA

WHAT THEY DID HERE. WHAT WE'RE DOING NOW.

From 1933 to 1942, more than thirty Civilian Conservation Corps camps operated in South Carolina. Young men from the Lowcountry, the Midlands, and the Upcountry planted, dug, hauled, and built. Almost every state park you took your family to as a kid was theirs first.

WHAT THEY BUILT IN SOUTH CAROLINA

THE STATE PARKS

CCC enrollees built the bones of South Carolina's state park system. Aiken, Barnwell, Cheraw, Chester, Givhans Ferry, Greenwood, Hunting Island, Kings Mountain, Lee, Myrtle Beach, Oconee, Paris Mountain, Poinsett, Sesquicentennial, and Table Rock were all carved, dug, and laid by hand during the CCC years.

They cleared trails. They built picnic shelters from local stone. They put up bathhouses, dam spillways, and ranger cabins that are still standing. Kings Mountain alone took years of CCC labor to shape from raw woods into a working park.

Hunting Island, where the lighthouse looks out over the Atlantic, was largely a CCC build. Myrtle Beach State Park, before the highway came through and the boardwalks went up, was CCC ground first.

THE FORESTS & THE SOIL

Francis Marion National Forest took the brunt of the hurricane of 1893 and never fully recovered. CCC crews replanted across the forest. They built fire towers, cut access roads, and dug ditches in the swamps for drainage. The forest you see today, the one Hugo nearly leveled in 1989 and that came back again, started its second life under CCC hands.

In the Upcountry, Sumter National Forest and the soil conservation districts of the Piedmont got CCC terracing work, kudzu plantings (which seemed like a good idea at the time), and erosion control across thousands of farms. The terraces are still there. So is the kudzu.

On the coast, CCC crews planted dune grass and built beach protection along the Grand Strand and the Lowcountry barrier islands. The hurricane prep we're doing now sits on top of work they started almost a century ago.

WHAT WE'RE DOING NOW

THE WORK CONTINUES.

The Connelly grandkids learned how to do hard times from our great-grandmother. She lived through the Great Depression. She made us save wrapping paper, finish our plates, and keep something squirreled away in a coffee can. She did not call it hardship. She called it taking care. The CCC Boys is what taking care looks like in 2026.

The corps is based in Colleton County. Justin and Jeremy Shearer and their cousin Joe Nettles, grandsons of Joseph Connelly, are building it from home. Same coast their grandparents knew. Same state parks they grew up in.

The work the corps is doing now: hurricane prep along the Atlantic and Gulf coasts so families and boats don't get caught flat-footed when the storm comes; shingle recycling in the Columbia metro to keep tear-offs out of landfills; marine apprenticeships in Walterboro; and a software portfolio built with AI as the partner, not the replacement.

The corps is worker-owned. Members draw a steady wage first, then operating costs, then runway, then the surplus goes back to the people who made it. Not shareholders. Same wage-first ordering the old CCC ran on. Same handshake.

A LOCAL CORPS, A NATIONAL MOVEMENT.

South Carolina is the first chapter. Other states are next, as builders raise their hands. The work is open source. The playbook is public. The brand is everyone's.
